**Version:** 2.0 (rebuilt for Vision & Mission v4.2; subscriber-success primary. Replaces the SaaS-era blueprint, which is archived.)

**Purpose:** Understanding who we serve and how to genuinely help them succeed

---

## Core Philosophy

The people we serve are **subscribers first, buyers second.** In the old model the customer was a user of a software tool, and success meant a product outcome. That is gone. The asset now is a trusted audience, and the primary relationship is with a subscribed viewer who trusts Jamie's honesty and judgement. Revenue is a consequence of that trust, not the point of contact.

So this blueprint defines success on **two tracks**, with the first leading:

1. **Subscriber success (primary).** Did the person get clearer signal in a noisy landscape, make better decisions, save time and money, and keep their own judgement sharp as AI accelerates? The outcome is trust earned and usefulness delivered, not a transaction completed.
2. **Buyer success (secondary, downstream).** For the smaller commercial slice who pay (independent benchmarks, advisory), did the work de-risk a real decision and deliver measurable value?

The job of this document is the same as ever: filter IN the right people, filter OUT the wrong ones, and understand how to create authentic value, but recalibrated for an audience-and-trust model rather than a product-sales model.

**On Collision Chamber.** A large part of Jamie's 2026 building focus is Collision Chamber (a trust-and-discrimination platform for human-and-AI knowledge, flagship-first as a philosophy journal). In this blueprint it is treated as the **flagship build-in-public vehicle for 2026**: the main source of proof and content that earns subscriber trust, and one downstream conversion path. It is not the subject of this blueprint. Collision Chamber has its own market, customers (authors, venues, labs), and monetisation, and gets its own product foundation documents separately.

## Section I: Ideal People We Serve

### Primary Focus Group: The Technical Builder / AI Engineer

**Who They Are:**
Software engineers, indie hackers, AI engineers, and senior individual contributors. Global, English-speaking. Employed at technology companies or building independently. Comfortable with code, tools, and the frontier.

**What Drives Them:**
Competence and relevance. They want to stay sharp and valuable as AI advances, not be quietly made replaceable. They are driven by what is true and what works, and repelled by hype and manufactured authority.

**How They Think:**
Evidence first. They distrust marketing, discount sponsored praise, and reward people who show their work and their code. They make decisions by testing, not by taking claims on faith. They will follow someone for years if the trust holds, and leave the moment it turns to hype.

**Where They Are:**
YouTube, X, GitHub, Hacker News, developer newsletters, technical podcasts, Discord and Reddit communities (r/LocalLLaMA, r/MachineLearning and similar).

**What They Care About:**
Honesty, verifiability, open source, and keeping their own judgement. They care whether a recommendation comes from someone who actually used the thing.

**How They Communicate:**
Direct, technical, low tolerance for fluff. They value specifics (numbers, repos, real examples) over adjectives. They share things that are genuinely useful or genuinely honest.

**What Frustrates Them:**
A new "best" tool every week, most of it sponsored. Signal lost in noise. The fear that leaning on AI is eroding their own skill and reducing them to a middleman between a model's output and a result.

### Secondary Focus Group: The Founder / Solopreneur Navigating AI

**Who They Are:**
Solo and small-team founders, and operators building with AI. Time-poor. Decision-makers for their own stack. Often technical-adjacent rather than deeply technical.

**What Drives Them:**
Making good calls on AI without a team or a consulting budget. They want practical truth and return on time and money, and they fear falling behind competitors who use AI well.

**How They Think:**
Pragmatic and outcome-focused. They want to know what to adopt and what to ignore, and they need proof before they invest scarce time or cash.

**Where They Are:**
X, LinkedIn, YouTube, founder and indie-hacker communities, business and AI podcasts, newsletters.

**What They Care About:**
Practical, trustworthy guidance from someone who has actually built, not theory from a guru or a consultant who never shipped.

**How They Communicate:**
Plain, busy, ROI-minded. They respond to clarity and to evidence that something works in the real world.

**What Frustrates Them:**
Hype-driven decisions, no trusted source, and the constant low-level fear of being left behind. Consultants are expensive and unproven; influencers are loud and unreliable.

### Tertiary Focus Group: Operators, Decision-makers, and Benchmark Buyers

**Who They Are:**
Heads of engineering and product, investors, and teams making build-or-buy decisions. They hold real budgets. This is the commercial slice, the track that converts trust into direct revenue.

**What Drives Them:**
De-risking decisions with independent, rigorous data. They are accountable for choices and want a neutral source they can defend.

**How They Think:**
Risk-aware and rigour-minded. They discount vendor marketing dressed as research and look for independence and a verifiable method.

**Where They Are:**
LinkedIn, industry newsletters and reports, podcasts, and through inbound referral once authority is established.

**What They Care About:**
Independence, methodological transparency, and accountability. They will pay for benchmarks and advisory that genuinely reduce risk.

**How They Communicate:**
Professional, careful, evidence-led. They want to see the method, not just the conclusion.

**What Frustrates Them:**
No independent benchmark they can trust. Vendor decks masquerading as analysis. Internal teams without frontier practitioner insight.

## Section II: Real Problems We Address

### Primary Focus Group Problems (Technical Builder / AI Engineer)

**Surface Problems:**
- Too much AI content, too little honest assessment of what actually works
- A new tool hyped every week, mostly through paid promotion
- Hard to know which verdicts to trust

**Root Cause Problems:**
- The incentives of AI media reward reach and hype over honesty, so the loudest voices are the least reliable
- Almost no one both builds at the frontier and tells the unvarnished truth, including their failures
- Signal-to-noise is collapsing as synthetic content and agent traffic rise

**Unrecognised Problems:**
- Quiet skill erosion from leaning on AI without keeping their own judgement sharp (the "middleman" effect)
- Slowly outsourcing thinking to the machine, one convenient default at a time
- Following sources that feel informative but are optimised for engagement, not truth

**Problem Impact:**
- Time and money wasted on tools that do not deliver
- Decisions made on hype, then regretted
- A growing, low-grade anxiety about relevance as AI advances, with no trusted guide

### Secondary Focus Group Problems (Founder / Solopreneur)

**Surface Problems:**
- Cannot tell which AI moves matter for their specific business
- No trusted advisor who has actually built
- Overwhelmed by options and noise

**Root Cause Problems:**
- Advice comes from people selling courses or consulting, not from practitioners with skin in the game
- Proof is anecdotal or marketing-led, so it is hard to justify investment
- They lack the time to test and compare for themselves

**Unrecognised Problems:**
- Adopting AI for appearance rather than genuine advantage
- Missing the few moves that would actually matter while chasing the visible ones
- Building on tools that will not last

**Problem Impact:**
- Wasted spend and wasted time they cannot spare
- Falling behind competitors who choose better
- Decision paralysis, or worse, confident wrong decisions

### Tertiary Focus Group Problems (Operators / Benchmark Buyers)

**Surface Problems:**
- No independent benchmark to trust for build-or-buy decisions
- Hard to evaluate AI vendors objectively
- Internal teams lack frontier practitioner insight

**Root Cause Problems:**
- The available "research" is largely vendor marketing with a conflict of interest
- Traditional analysts are slow and often lack hands-on building experience
- There is no neutral, transparent, method-led source in most verticals

**Unrecognised Problems:**
- Betting budgets on vendor claims that have never been independently tested
- Treating a model monoculture as settled fact rather than an open question
- Underweighting how fast the frontier moves under their decisions

**Problem Impact:**
- Expensive wrong bets that are hard to reverse
- Accountability exposure for decisions made on weak evidence
- Slower, less confident decision-making

## Section III: People Filtering Criteria

### Who We Should Focus On

**Positive Indicators:**
- They value honesty over hype and reward people who show their work
- They are builders or decision-makers who actually act on what they learn
- They want to keep their own judgement sharp, not outsource their thinking
- They can verify claims (read code, check numbers) and respect that they can

**Green Flags:**
- They engage with the substance (ask about method, repos, real results), not just the headline
- They share content because it is useful or honest, not because it is hyped
- They stay through the unglamorous parts of a build, not just the wins
- They respond well to a shared failure or a public post-mortem

### Who We Should Avoid

**Negative Indicators:**
- They want hype, hot takes, and get-rich-quick shortcuts
- They want to be told what to think, not helped to think better
- They are hostile to AI-assisted work on principle, or treat any AI use as cheating
- They will not act, only collect content

**Red Flags:**
- They demand certainty and dislike honest "it depends" or "this failed"
- They punish disclosed sponsorship while rewarding hidden promotion elsewhere
- They want free, bespoke consulting under the guise of audience engagement
- They are drawn to the persona, not the substance

## Section IV: Success Definitions

Success leads with the subscriber. The buyer track follows.

### Primary Focus Group Success (Technical Builder / AI Engineer) - SUBSCRIBER SUCCESS

**Their Definition of Success:**
They found a source they can trust in a noisy field, and they make better, faster decisions about AI because of it, while keeping their own judgement sharp.

**Success Outcomes They Want:**
- Clear signal: they know what actually works and what to ignore
- Time and money saved by skipping tools that do not deliver
- Confidence that the verdicts they act on are honest
- A sense that they are getting more capable as AI advances, not less

**Success Indicators They Recognise:**
- They subscribe, return, and treat Jamie as a primary source
- They act on a recommendation and it pays off
- They share the work with peers
- They feel less hype fatigue and more clarity

**Success Indicators They Don't Recognise:**
- Their own judgement is strengthening because the content models how to think, not just what to think
- They are avoiding expensive mistakes they never see (the lessons Jamie already paid for)
- They are building a durable trust relationship that will matter more, not less, as AI noise grows

**Success Timeline:**
- **Immediate (first visit):** they get one honest, useful answer and sense this is different
- **Short-term (weeks):** they subscribe and return; cadence earns a habit
- **Medium-term (months):** they trust Jamie enough to act on his verdicts and skip their own testing
- **Long-term (1 year+):** Jamie is their primary source for navigating AI change
- **Ultimate (years):** the trust compounds into advocacy and, for some, conversion to paid offerings or participation in builds like Collision Chamber

### Secondary Focus Group Success (Founder / Solopreneur) - SUBSCRIBER SUCCESS

**Their Definition of Success:**
They make confident, correct AI decisions for their business without a team or a consulting budget, because they have a practitioner they trust.

**Success Outcomes They Want:**
- Knowing what to adopt and what to ignore for their situation
- Proof before they invest scarce time and money
- Not falling behind competitors who use AI well

**Success Indicators They Recognise:**
- They make an AI decision based on Jamie's work and it pays off
- They subscribe and bring questions
- They feel less behind and more in control

**Success Indicators They Don't Recognise:**
- They are avoiding costly wrong bets they would otherwise have made
- They are learning to evaluate AI moves themselves, not just follow

**Success Timeline:**
- **Short-term:** one decision improved by the content
- **Medium-term:** they rely on Jamie as a sounding board for AI calls
- **Long-term:** they convert to a paid offering or become an advisory inbound

### Tertiary Focus Group Success (Operators / Benchmark Buyers) - BUYER SUCCESS

**Their Definition of Success:**
A real build-or-buy or vendor decision was de-risked by independent, transparent evidence, and it held up.

**Success Outcomes They Want:**
- A neutral, method-led benchmark they can defend internally
- Frontier practitioner insight their team lacks
- Measurable reduction in decision risk

**Success Indicators They Recognise:**
- They subscribe to a benchmark or commission advisory
- The decision it informed proved correct
- They return and refer peers

**Success Indicators They Don't Recognise:**
- They are building a relationship with the most trusted independent source in the vertical before competitors do
- The transparency of the method is itself de-risking their accountability

**Success Timeline:**
- **Short-term:** one decision informed by a benchmark or advisory session
- **Medium-term:** a subscription or repeat engagement
- **Long-term:** a standing relationship and referrals; the commercial backbone of the model

## Section VII: Relationship Framework

### Relationship Philosophy

**Our Approach to Relationships:**
The relationship is built on earned trust, not transactions. The default posture is to give: honest assessments, open code, useful insight, before any money changes hands. Trust is the asset; everything monetisable is a consequence of it.

**Helping Without Selling:**
- Publish honest verdicts and open code freely, including for tools Jamie has no stake in
- Tell people what does not work so they can skip the expensive lesson
- Help them make a good decision even if it points away from anything Jamie offers
- Share failures and post-mortems openly

### Trust Building Approach

**Demonstrating Competence:**
Show the building, not the talking: real products, real code, the live Collision Chamber build through 2026.

**Showing Genuine Care:**
Warn people off things that will waste their time or money. Answer the real question, not the one that sells.

**Proving Our Difference:**
Disclose every sponsorship and keep the verdict honest anyway. Publish the kills. Open the code.

**Building Confidence:**
Consistency over time. Frequency before reach. The back-catalogue and the track record do the persuading.

### Boundary and Filtering Framework

**Identifying Good Fit:**
People who value honesty, act on what they learn, and want sharper judgement rather than someone to think for them.

**Graceful Redirection:**
Point poor-fit people to better-suited sources without disdain. Saying "I am not the right source for that" is itself trustworthy.

**Maintaining Focus:**
Serve builders and founders navigating AI honestly. Do not chase hype audiences or vanity reach that the model cannot convert to trust.

**Saying No Helpfully:**
Decline free bespoke consulting and hype demands while still leaving the person better informed than they arrived.

## Section VIII: Implementation Guidance

### Content and Education Strategy

**What to Teach:**
What actually works at the frontier and why; how to evaluate AI tools and claims; how to keep your own judgement sharp as AI gets more capable. Teach how to think, not only what to think.

**How to Demonstrate Value:**
Build in public. In 2026, the spine of the content is the Collision Chamber build: the most ambitious AI-native thing Jamie is making, shown honestly, wins and failures alike. Building it IS the sharing engine, which is how a solo operator with a full-time job sustains cadence without running two separate efforts.

**Problem Recognition Help:**
Name the signal-versus-noise problem and the skill-erosion risk plainly, so people recognise what they are actually struggling with.

**Solution Confidence Building:**
Show the working. Open the code. Publish the method. Let people verify rather than trust blindly.

### Community and Connection Building

**Finding Ideal People:**
Where builders and founders already are: YouTube, X, GitHub, Hacker News, technical and founder communities, podcasts (guesting for borrowed reach).

**Relationship Building:**
Engage honestly in comments and replies. Open-source the code so people can use and fork it. Let the work attract rather than chasing.

**Mutual Value Creation:**
Subscribers get clarity and sharper judgement; Jamie gets a trusted audience, feedback, and a body of proof. Some subscribers become Collision Chamber contributors or customers, which feeds both the mission and the business.

**Long-term Development:**
Frequency before reach. Consistency compounds trust. Protect the honesty line under sponsorship pressure, because it is the whole moat.

### Success Measurement

**Genuine Help Indicators:**
People act on the work and report it helped; they return; they refer peers.

**Communication Resonance:**
Engagement that is substantive (questions about method, shared failures landing well), not just vanity metrics.

**Mutual Benefit Signs:**
Growing subscribed audience, inbound opportunities (speaking, advisory, collaboration), and trust surviving honest failures.

**Authentic Value Metrics:**
Subscriber growth and retention, engagement quality, inbound opportunity count, and publishing cadence held. Not MRR or seat counts.

## Section IX: Business Integration

### Content and Product Design Guidance

**What to Build and Show:**
Prioritise builds that are honest, frontier, and worth watching. Collision Chamber is the 2026 flagship because it is ambitious, demonstrates the thesis, and generates a year of content. Convenience SaaS and benchmarks follow from real building, not from a product roadmap.

**Prioritisation:**
Rank by: does it earn trust, does it produce honest content, does it keep judgement sharp, and could it convert to a revenue line later (benchmarks, advisory) without compromising honesty.

**Experience Design:**
Video-first, verifiable, owned-channel-anchored. Make the code and the method inspectable.

### Marketing Guidance

**Message Development:**
Lead with the honest verdict and the proof, never the hype. "The field report from someone actually in the water. Tested, honest, useful."

**Channel Selection:**
YouTube primary; jamiewatters.work owned home; X and LinkedIn for distribution; podcasts for borrowed reach.

**Content Strategy:**
The Collision Chamber build as the 2026 spine, supported by honest tool verdicts and public post-mortems as signature formats. Frequency before reach.

### Sales and Conversion Guidance

**Qualification Criteria:**
For the buyer track, look for operators and teams with real decisions and budgets who value independence. For subscribers, there is no qualification; the funnel is trust, and conversion to paid is a downstream consequence.

**Conversation Framework:**
Help them make the right decision first, even if it is not with Jamie. Sell by having been useful, not by pitching.

**Decision Support:**
Give buyers the method and the evidence, and let it stand on its own. Transparency is the close.

### Support and Relationship Maintenance

**Success Delivery:**
For subscribers, keep delivering honest, useful work consistently. For buyers, deliver benchmarks and advisory that hold up under scrutiny.

**Relationship Maintenance:**
Stay honest, stay consistent, keep disclosing, keep shipping. The day the work turns to hype, the trust and the model break together.

**Continuous Improvement:**
Review quarterly against real audience and engagement data. Update segments and proof points as new builds ship and fail. Watch for the audience-broadening pull of Collision Chamber (toward researchers and the knowledge-and-meaning crowd) and decide deliberately whether to embrace or keep it peripheral.
