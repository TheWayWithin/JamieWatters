I just realized my "build in public" site was failing the very people I want to serve.

Here's the uncomfortable truth:

A chronological blog feed isn't a resource.
It's a scavenger hunt.

If someone wants to learn from my experience building Trader-7, they have to scroll through 47 unrelated posts hoping to find the relevant ones.

That's not helpful. That's lazy architecture.

So I'm redesigning everything.

The new structure:

→ Projects as case studies (status, metrics, timeline, tech stack)
→ Failures documented prominently (not buried in random posts)
→ Content categorized by who it serves (not just when it was written)
→ Multiple entry points for different learning styles

The meta-lesson?

Your build-in-public site isn't a diary.
It's a product.
And like any product, it needs to solve a real problem for a specific user.

Most founders write for themselves.
The good ones write for the people they're trying to help.

I was doing the former. Time to fix that.

Full breakdown of the 30-hour sprint I've planned: jamiewatters.work/journey

What's your take - is your content organized for YOU or for your audience?
